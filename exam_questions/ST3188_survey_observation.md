# ST3188 — Survey & Observation Methods: 50 Exam Questions with Model Answers

---

## Part A: Survey Methodology (Q1-10)

### Q1. What is survey research?
**Model answer:** Systematic collection of data from a sample through structured questions. Aims to describe characteristics, opinions, or behaviours of a population. Relies on representative sampling, valid measurement, and unbiased administration to support generalisation.

### Q2. State the main survey administration modes.
**Model answer:** (1) Face-to-face (in-person interview). (2) Telephone (CATI — Computer-Assisted Telephone Interviewing). (3) Mail/postal. (4) Web/online (CAWI). (5) Mobile/SMS. (6) Mixed-mode. Each has trade-offs in cost, speed, response rate, and data quality.

### Q3. Compare face-to-face vs online surveys.
**Model answer:** Face-to-face: high response rate, can probe, complex tasks; expensive, slow, interviewer bias. Online: cheap, fast, self-paced, anonymous, multimedia possible; lower response, self-selection bias (tech-adept), no probing. Choice depends on budget, audience, and question complexity.

### Q4. What is mixed-mode surveying and why use it?
**Model answer:** Combining multiple modes in one study (e.g., web first, non-responders follow-up by phone or mail). Benefits: increases response rates, covers more population segments, balances cost. Challenges: mode effects (responses differ across modes), analysis complexity. Common in large national surveys.

### Q5. Describe a CATI system.
**Model answer:** Computer-Assisted Telephone Interviewing. Interviewer reads questions from computer screen, enters responses directly. Benefits: automatic skip logic, validation at entry, call management, quality monitoring. Infrastructure: call centre, random-digit-dial, multilingual interviewers.

### Q6. What is a diary study?
**Model answer:** Participants record behaviours/events over time in diary format (paper, electronic). Used for activities like media consumption, product use, expenditure. Rich data but high participant burden. Attrition and compliance key issues. Often includes reminders and incentives.

### Q7. Distinguish panel and tracking studies.
**Model answer:** Panel: same participants observed repeatedly over time. Measures individual change. Tracking: different samples from same population over time. Measures aggregate change. Panels more powerful for causality; tracking more resistant to panel conditioning effects.

### Q8. Define survey response rate and how it's calculated.
**Model answer:** Response rate = completed interviews / eligible sample × 100%. Eligibility must be defined (e.g., business in target sector, adult 18+). Higher response rates reduce non-response bias risk. Benchmarks: >70% excellent; 50-70% good; <30% concerning for inference.

### Q9. Name the main survey errors.
**Model answer:** Total Survey Error framework: (1) Sampling error — variability from observing subset. (2) Coverage error — sampling frame misses target units. (3) Non-response error — missed units differ systematically. (4) Measurement error — respondent or interviewer errors, question effects. (5) Processing error — coding, data entry.

### Q10. Describe satisficing.
**Model answer:** Respondents giving "good enough" answers rather than optimal ones, to reduce cognitive effort. Manifests: straightlining (same answer across items), non-differentiation, premature closure ("don't know"), random responses to long scales. Mitigate: short surveys, clear questions, attention checks, incentivised compliance.

## Part B: Questionnaire Design Principles (Q11-20)

### Q11. State the main rules for question wording.
**Model answer:** (1) Clear, simple language — avoid jargon. (2) One idea per question — no double-barrelled. (3) No leading or loaded phrasing. (4) Avoid negative phrasing (causes errors). (5) Define ambiguous terms. (6) Specify timeframes. (7) Use respondent's vocabulary. (8) Pilot test to detect misunderstanding.

### Q12. Define a double-barrelled question and give an example.
**Model answer:** A question that asks about two distinct issues in one item. Example: "How satisfied are you with the product and service?" Respondent cannot answer if they feel differently about each. Remedy: split into two questions — "Rate satisfaction with the product" AND "Rate satisfaction with the service."

### Q13. Define a leading question and give an example.
**Model answer:** A question that suggests a particular answer. Example: "Don't you agree that higher taxes hurt the economy?" Remedy: neutral wording — "What is your view on the impact of higher taxes on the economy?" Or balanced alternatives: "To what extent do you agree or disagree...?"

### Q14. Define response bias.
**Model answer:** Systematic tendency for respondents to answer in particular ways regardless of truth. Types: (1) Social desirability — responding to look good. (2) Acquiescence — agreeing with statements. (3) Extreme response — using scale endpoints. (4) Central tendency — avoiding endpoints. (5) Order effects. Mitigate with varied item formats, reverse-scored items, anonymity.

### Q15. What is social desirability bias?
**Model answer:** Respondents answer in ways they perceive as socially acceptable rather than truthfully. Strongest for sensitive topics (income, sex, drugs, prejudice). Remedies: (1) Anonymity. (2) Indirect questioning. (3) Randomised response techniques. (4) List experiments. (5) Self-administered modes. (6) Confidentiality assurances.

### Q16. Distinguish open-ended vs closed-ended questions.
**Model answer:** Open: respondents provide own answer (e.g., "Why did you switch brands?"). Rich qualitative data; hard to analyse at scale. Closed: fixed response options (multiple choice, Likert). Easy to code and analyse; constrained responses. Use open for exploration, closed for measurement.

### Q17. Describe best practices for ordering survey questions.
**Model answer:** (1) Start with easy, engaging questions to build rapport. (2) Group by topic (grouping aids respondent comprehension). (3) Sensitive questions later. (4) Demographics at end (not beginning — avoid priming). (5) Randomise order within question groups to control order effects. (6) End with thank-you and contact info.

### Q18. What are screeners?
**Model answer:** Eligibility questions at the start of a survey to identify qualified respondents. Example: "Have you bought our product in last 6 months?" Filters out ineligible respondents. Used to control sample composition and reduce ineligible completion.

### Q19. Describe skip logic.
**Model answer:** Conditional question routing based on previous answers. Example: if "No" to "Do you drink coffee?", skip coffee consumption follow-ups. Tailors survey to respondent, reduces burden, maintains flow. Online/CATI systems handle automatically; paper requires clear instructions.

### Q20. What is a pilot study?
**Model answer:** Small-scale pre-test of the survey before full launch. Tests question wording, timing, flow, technical issues. Sample: 10-30 representative respondents. Identify problems: confusing questions, missing response options, overlong surveys. Pre-launch pilot is essential; fixes are cheap before and expensive after deployment.

## Part C: Observation Methods (Q21-30)

### Q21. Define observation research.
**Model answer:** Systematic recording of behaviour as it naturally occurs, without asking directly. Methods: direct human observation, video, electronic tracking. Strengths: unobtrusive, no respondent bias, behavioural reality. Weaknesses: observer bias, interpretation, ethics, cannot observe internal states.

### Q22. Distinguish structured vs unstructured observation.
**Model answer:** Structured: pre-defined categories for what to record (e.g., counts of specific behaviours). Quantitative focus. Unstructured: open description; qualitative, exploratory. Use structured when hypotheses are clear; unstructured for discovery. Most studies mix both.

### Q23. Distinguish participant vs non-participant observation.
**Model answer:** Participant: researcher is part of the group being observed (ethnography). Deep understanding; potential bias from involvement. Non-participant: researcher observes from outside. Less reactive but may miss context. Mixed roles ("participant-observer") common.

### Q24. Compare overt vs covert observation.
**Model answer:** Overt: observed know they're being observed. Ethical but subject to Hawthorne effect. Covert: observed unaware. More natural behaviour but ethical concerns — informed consent? Privacy? IRBs strictly review covert observation; justifiable for some public-setting research.

### Q25. What are mechanical/electronic observation methods?
**Model answer:** Devices that record behaviour automatically. Examples: traffic counters, scanner data (purchases), eye-tracking (gaze), clickstreams (web), GPS (location), accelerometers (physical activity). Advantages: objective, continuous, non-reactive. Limitations: context, motivation not captured.

### Q26. Describe eye-tracking research.
**Model answer:** Measures what people look at and for how long, using infrared cameras. Outputs: fixations, saccades, heatmaps, gaze paths. Applications: web design, advertising, packaging. Insights into attention and visual hierarchy. Expensive; controlled settings; but reveals what respondents cannot articulate.

### Q27. What is a mystery shopper study?
**Model answer:** Trained observer poses as customer to evaluate service quality, standard compliance, sales behaviour. Data: detailed rating forms. Widely used in retail, banking, hospitality. Provides actionable feedback. Must maintain realism; standardise observer ratings for reliability.

### Q28. Describe physiological measurement.
**Model answer:** Recording bodily responses: heart rate, skin conductance, EEG (brain activity), facial expression. Reveal emotional/physiological reactions below conscious awareness. Used in advertising, UX research. Limitations: expensive, lab-bound, interpretation requires expertise.

### Q29. Compare observation vs survey data.
**Model answer:** Observation: actual behaviour, no recall/memory issues, bias from misreporting. But cannot access motivations, opinions. Survey: captures subjective attitudes and reported behaviour. Subject to recall, social desirability. Complementary: use observation for what people do, surveys for why. Triangulate for full picture.

### Q30. What is a garbology study?
**Model answer:** Analysing household refuse to measure actual consumption (not self-reported). Reveals what people actually consume vs what they report. Example: finding more alcohol bottles than reported in surveys. Now ethically/logistically limited; historical interest but rarely used today.

## Part D: Response Bias and Measurement (Q31-40)

### Q31. Describe acquiescence bias.
**Model answer:** Tendency to agree with survey items regardless of content. Particularly problematic with agree/disagree scales. Mitigate: (1) Balance positive and negative statements. (2) Reverse-scored items. (3) Vary response format (some items use bipolar scales). (4) Avoid double-negatives. Check reverse-coded items for consistency.

### Q32. Describe central tendency bias.
**Model answer:** Tendency to avoid extreme response options (strongly agree, strongly disagree). Compresses responses into the middle. Especially strong in East Asian cultures. Remedies: (1) Use bipolar scales. (2) Describe extreme options as reasonable (not absolute). (3) Compare to reference cases.

### Q33. Describe straightlining.
**Model answer:** Giving the same answer to a series of items without reading. Common in long surveys with matrix questions. Detection: check for identical responses across dissimilar items. Remedies: (1) Shorter surveys. (2) Break up matrices. (3) Reverse-coded items to catch. (4) Attention checks ("Please select 'Strongly Disagree' to show you're reading").

### Q34. What are attention checks?
**Model answer:** Items designed to verify participants are reading carefully. Examples: "For quality, please select 'Yes'." Items that fail are flagged or removed. Essential in online panels where click-farming is a risk. Increases data quality substantially.

### Q35. Describe primacy and recency effects.
**Model answer:** In aural presentation (phone), respondents more likely to choose last option (recency). In visual (web, paper), more likely to choose first option (primacy). Remedies: (1) Randomise response order. (2) Be aware of mode. (3) Design questions so order doesn't matter substantively.

### Q36. What is question-order bias?
**Model answer:** Earlier questions influence later answers. Example: asking about happiness after discussing life dissatisfaction reduces happiness ratings. Remedies: (1) Randomise question order where possible. (2) Place sensitive questions later. (3) Pilot test orderings. (4) Consider contextual influences in interpretation.

### Q37. Describe Dillman's Tailored Design Method.
**Model answer:** Evidence-based approach to maximise response rates. Principles: (1) Multiple contacts. (2) Personalised communication. (3) Clear benefits. (4) Professional appearance. (5) Easy response process. (6) Incentives. Demonstrated to improve response rates 20-40 points over minimal efforts.

### Q38. Distinguish item-level vs unit-level non-response.
**Model answer:** Unit-level: entire survey not completed (participant refuses). Item-level: some questions unanswered within a completed survey (e.g., income often missing). Handle differently: unit — weighting adjustment; item — imputation (mean, regression, multiple imputation).

### Q39. Define recall bias.
**Model answer:** Systematic error in retrospective reports. Memory fades, especially for ordinary events. Bias direction often underestimates unmemorable events. Mitigate: (1) Short recall periods. (2) Concrete anchors ("Since New Year..."). (3) Diary methods. (4) Electronic logs. Diaries prevent recall bias at cost of participant burden.

### Q40. Describe telescoping.
**Model answer:** Mislocation of events in time: forward telescoping — events remembered as more recent than they were; backward — older. Biases frequency estimates. Mitigate: use bounded recall (reference to a prior interview), or shorter recall periods. Critical for behaviour frequency questions (e.g., doctor visits).

## Part E: Data Quality and Analysis (Q41-45)

### Q41. How to identify low-quality survey responses?
**Model answer:** Flags: (1) Completion time unrealistically fast. (2) Straightlining on matrices. (3) Identical answers in reverse-coded pairs. (4) Failed attention checks. (5) Nonsensical open-ended responses. (6) Impossible demographic combinations. Remove or flag and analyse both with/without. Report what was excluded.

### Q42. How to handle item non-response in analysis?
**Model answer:** (1) Listwise deletion: remove cases with any missing. Simple; loses data. (2) Pairwise deletion: use available for each analysis. Inconsistent sample sizes. (3) Mean imputation: replace with mean. Underestimates variance. (4) Regression/multiple imputation: predict missing from observed. Preserves variance. (5) Model-based (full-information ML, EM). Gold standard for MAR missingness.

### Q43. What is MCAR, MAR, MNAR?
**Model answer:** Missing Completely At Random (MCAR): missingness independent of observed and unobserved. Missing At Random (MAR): depends only on observed. Can be addressed with imputation based on observed. Missing Not At Random (MNAR): depends on the unobserved value itself (e.g., high earners hiding income). Requires sensitivity analysis or pattern-mixture models.

### Q44. Describe survey weighting.
**Model answer:** Assigning weights to respondents to compensate for unequal selection probabilities and non-response. Types: design weights (1/selection prob), non-response weights, post-stratification weights. Final weight = product. Increases SE but reduces bias. Common in large surveys (NHANES, BHPS).

### Q45. What is mode effect in surveys?
**Model answer:** Different survey modes (phone, mail, web) produce different responses even for same questions. Examples: face-to-face produces more socially desirable answers than online. Detected by mode comparison studies. Important in mixed-mode surveys — data must be analysed accounting for mode.

## Part F: Application (Q46-50)

### Q46. Design a survey to measure customer satisfaction for a software company.
**Model answer:** (1) Target: active users, stratified by license type. (2) Mode: email-invited web survey; mobile-optimised. (3) Length: 8-10 minutes max. (4) Questions: (a) NPS (0-10), (b) Specific satisfaction on Likert scales (features, support, value), (c) Open-ended "biggest pain point", (d) Behavioural (usage frequency), (e) Demographics (role, industry). (5) Pilot with 30 users. (6) Multiple contacts: initial + 2 reminders. (7) Analyse by segment. (8) Report response rate, demographics, and NPS benchmark.

### Q47. You want to study actual cell phone use patterns. Survey or observation?
**Model answer:** Combined approach. Self-report surveys underestimate actual use (recall error). Deploy: (1) Mobile activity tracking app (observational) — objective usage data. (2) Brief survey for demographics, motivations, subjective experience. (3) Reconcile: compare reported vs actual use. Requires IRB approval for tracking; clear informed consent. Triangulation provides both quantitative actuals and subjective context.

### Q48. A retailer wants to evaluate store layout's effect on browsing. Design.
**Model answer:** Observation study: (1) Structured observation with trained mystery shoppers or (ethically-approved) video analysis. (2) Metrics: time in store, sections visited, items examined, purchase decisions. (3) A/B test: alter layout in experimental stores, compare to control stores. (4) Complement with post-visit survey for satisfaction and intent. (5) Analysis: layout effect on key metrics; heterogeneous effects by customer segment. (6) Ethics: clear signage about observation, data anonymisation.

### Q49. Critique a survey with 50-item grid on a single screen and 10% response rate.
**Model answer:** Critique: (1) 50-item grid causes straightlining — responses are low quality. (2) 10% response rate invites non-response bias — who completes such a long survey? (3) Fatigue, break items into multiple pages. (4) Mix question formats to avoid matrix fatigue. Recommendations: (i) shorten to 15-20 items, (ii) use progress bars and chunked layout, (iii) attention checks, (iv) pre-screen with key demographics, (v) target 30-40% response rate through better design and compensation. Current data likely unreliable.

### Q50. Your online survey panel's demographics don't match the UK population (overrepresents young, tech-savvy). How to handle?
**Model answer:** Recognise self-selection bias. (1) Apply post-stratification weights to match population on age, gender, region, education. (2) Conduct sensitivity analyses — do conclusions hold under different weightings? (3) Acknowledge in limitations: results may not generalise to older or less digital-literate populations. (4) Complement with other modes for robustness (phone, paper). (5) If cost-constrained, triangulate with external benchmarks (government statistics, ONS). (6) Report: weighted and unweighted results for transparency.

---

**Exam tip:** For survey/observation questions, always: (1) state target population and sampling method, (2) justify mode choice, (3) evaluate questionnaire design against bias risks, (4) discuss response rate expectations and how to maximise, (5) describe observation protocols rigorously for reliability, (6) address ethics and data quality explicitly.
