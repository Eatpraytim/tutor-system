# ST3188 — Qualitative Research & Secondary Data: 50 Exam Questions with Model Answers

---

## Part A: Qualitative Research Fundamentals (Q1-10)

### Q1. Define qualitative research.
**Model answer:** Research that explores meanings, experiences, and contexts through non-numeric data (words, images, observations). Uses small, purposive samples and open-ended methods. Aims for depth and understanding, not statistical generalisation. Contrasts with quantitative's numerical focus.

### Q2. Compare qualitative and quantitative research.
**Model answer:** Qualitative: interpretive, inductive, small samples, rich data, descriptive. Explores "why" and "how". Quantitative: positivist, deductive, large samples, structured data, statistical. Tests hypotheses, measures "how much". Complementary — mixed methods combine both.

### Q3. When is qualitative research appropriate?
**Model answer:** (1) Exploratory research where concepts are not yet defined. (2) Complex phenomena requiring contextual understanding. (3) Sensitive topics where open discussion is essential. (4) Hypothesis generation. (5) Evaluating experiences or motivations. (6) Understanding minority or under-studied groups.

### Q4. State the main qualitative methods.
**Model answer:** (1) Depth interviews — one-on-one open-ended conversations. (2) Focus groups — small group discussions (6-10 participants). (3) Ethnography — immersive observation. (4) Case study — in-depth examination of one or few cases. (5) Content analysis — systematic analysis of text/images. (6) Grounded theory — iterative coding to build theory.

### Q5. Describe depth interviews.
**Model answer:** One-on-one, semi-structured or unstructured conversations exploring personal experiences, motivations, opinions. Duration: 30-120 minutes typically. Audio-recorded and transcribed. Advantages: depth, flexibility, sensitive topic access. Weaknesses: time-intensive, interviewer effects, small samples.

### Q6. Describe focus groups.
**Model answer:** Moderated group discussions (6-10 participants) on a specific topic. Duration: 60-90 minutes. Moderator guides using semi-structured guide. Advantages: efficient, group dynamics reveal consensus/disagreement, spontaneous insights. Weaknesses: dominant voices, social desirability, groupthink.

### Q7. When to use focus groups vs depth interviews?
**Model answer:** Focus groups: when group interaction adds value (brainstorming, testing reactions, exploring norms). Depth interviews: for sensitive topics, complex individual journeys, busy elites. Mixed approach often ideal: focus groups to map territory, depth interviews to probe deeper.

### Q8. What is ethnography?
**Model answer:** Immersive observation of people in their natural context over extended periods. Researcher integrates with the setting. Provides rich contextual understanding. Common in anthropology, consumer behaviour research. Time-intensive; subject to researcher bias and observer effects.

### Q9. Define grounded theory.
**Model answer:** Systematic inductive method where theory emerges from data rather than being tested. Iterative: collect → code → analyse → collect more. Coding stages: open coding (initial), axial coding (linking), selective coding (core category). Useful for building theories in under-researched areas.

### Q10. What are the three criteria for qualitative rigour?
**Model answer:** (1) Credibility (analogous to internal validity) — accurate representation of participants' views. (2) Transferability (external validity) — applicability to other contexts. (3) Dependability (reliability) — consistency across researchers/time. Plus confirmability — neutrality and lack of researcher bias.

## Part B: Qualitative Data Analysis (Q11-20)

### Q11. Describe thematic analysis.
**Model answer:** Systematic identification and analysis of patterns (themes) in qualitative data. Braun & Clarke's six phases: (1) Familiarisation with data. (2) Generate initial codes. (3) Search for themes. (4) Review themes. (5) Define and name themes. (6) Produce report. Flexible across qualitative traditions.

### Q12. What is coding in qualitative research?
**Model answer:** Assigning descriptive labels (codes) to segments of text. First-order codes describe what's said; second-order themes aggregate codes. Codes capture recurring concepts, contradictions, causes. Iterative refinement: codes evolve with deeper analysis.

### Q13. Distinguish a priori and emergent codes.
**Model answer:** A priori codes: derived from theory or research questions before analysis. Emergent codes: arise from the data during analysis. Most qualitative research uses a mix — a priori framework with emergent additions. Entirely emergent = pure grounded theory.

### Q14. What is inter-rater reliability in qualitative coding?
**Model answer:** Agreement between multiple coders applying the same codes. Measured by Cohen's κ or percent agreement. Supports coding consistency. Discuss disagreements, refine coding scheme until acceptable reliability (often κ > 0.7). Controversial — some traditions reject inter-rater reliability as positivist.

### Q15. What is triangulation in qualitative research?
**Model answer:** Using multiple methods, sources, or investigators to cross-validate findings. (1) Data triangulation — multiple sources (interviews + documents + observation). (2) Investigator triangulation — multiple researchers code independently. (3) Method triangulation — qualitative + quantitative. (4) Theoretical triangulation — multiple theoretical lenses. Strengthens credibility.

### Q16. Describe member checking.
**Model answer:** Returning findings to participants for verification — do they recognise your interpretation of their experience? Enhances credibility. Methods: share transcripts, themes, reports. Participants can correct misrepresentation. Challenge: participants may disagree due to sensitive material or defensive response.

### Q17. What is reflexivity?
**Model answer:** Researcher's awareness of their own influence on data collection and interpretation. Acknowledges biases, assumptions, position. Discussed in methods section. Examples: positionality statement, reflective journal, checking with critical friend. Key to demonstrating rigour.

### Q18. What is saturation?
**Model answer:** Point at which additional data collection yields no new insights. Theoretical saturation: no new theoretical concepts; data saturation: no new information. Guides sample size in qualitative research. Often reached at 12-30 interviews, but depends on diversity of participants and complexity of topic.

### Q19. Compare deductive vs inductive coding.
**Model answer:** Deductive: start with theory-driven codes, apply to data. Top-down. Inductive: codes emerge from data. Bottom-up (grounded theory style). Pragmatic approach: both. Frame research with existing theory while remaining open to new insights.

### Q20. Describe CAQDAS (Computer-Assisted Qualitative Data Analysis Software).
**Model answer:** Tools like NVivo, ATLAS.ti, MAXQDA for managing and coding large qualitative datasets. Features: code organisation, text search, query building, visualisation. Assists but doesn't replace interpretation. Useful for large studies; overkill for small projects.

## Part C: Secondary Data (Q21-30)

### Q21. Define secondary data.
**Model answer:** Data previously collected for other purposes but used to answer a current research question. Sources: government statistics, academic databases, business records, digital traces (web analytics, transactions). Contrasts with primary data collected specifically for the study.

### Q22. List the main types of secondary data sources.
**Model answer:** (1) Internal — company's own records, transactions, CRM. (2) External: (a) Government — census, economic indicators, open data. (b) Syndicated — commercial panels (Nielsen, IRI). (c) Database vendors — commercial databases. (d) Academic repositories. (e) Digital — web scraping, APIs, social media.

### Q23. State advantages of secondary data.
**Model answer:** (1) Cost-effective — no collection costs. (2) Fast — data exists. (3) Unobtrusive — no participant burden. (4) Often large scale (e.g., census). (5) Provides historical context. (6) Useful for validating primary data. (7) Ethical issues minimised (usually pre-anonymised).

### Q24. State limitations of secondary data.
**Model answer:** (1) Fit — may not match research question. (2) Currency — may be outdated. (3) Accuracy — unknown quality. (4) Specificity — aggregated categories. (5) Missing variables — cannot add questions. (6) Units of analysis may differ. (7) Comparability over time if definitions changed.

### Q25. Distinguish data sets you can "buy" vs "find".
**Model answer:** Buy: commercial products (Nielsen scanner data, Experian, Bloomberg) — expensive but rich. Find: free sources (government open data, academic repositories, web scraping). Evaluate both for relevance, quality, licensing terms. Budget-constrained research should prioritise free sources.

### Q26. Describe the criteria for evaluating secondary data quality.
**Model answer:** (1) Purpose — why originally collected. (2) Source credibility. (3) Currency — when collected. (4) Representativeness — sample/population coverage. (5) Variable definitions — match your constructs. (6) Missing data. (7) Granularity — level of detail. (8) Quality controls on original collection.

### Q27. What is data scraping?
**Model answer:** Automated extraction of data from websites or digital platforms. Tools: Python (BeautifulSoup, Scrapy), R, dedicated apps. Useful for: pricing data, reviews, social media, public records. Legal/ethical considerations: Terms of Service, robots.txt, copyright, GDPR.

### Q28. Describe syndicated research services.
**Model answer:** Commercial firms collecting and selling standardised data across many clients. Examples: Nielsen (retail scanner, TV audience), IRI, YouGov, Mintel. Advantages: consistent methodology, broad coverage, benchmarking. Cost: subscriptions range from thousands to millions annually.

### Q29. Define data fusion.
**Model answer:** Combining multiple data sources to create a richer dataset. Example: integrating customer CRM with third-party demographics; merging survey data with behavioural logs. Techniques: record linkage (exact matching), statistical matching (based on common variables), imputation. Ethical issues: privacy, re-identification risks.

### Q30. What are big data characteristics (3 Vs / 5 Vs)?
**Model answer:** (1) Volume — size of data. (2) Velocity — speed of generation/processing. (3) Variety — different types (structured, unstructured). Extended: (4) Veracity — quality/uncertainty. (5) Value — actionable insights. Contrasts with traditional structured datasets; requires distributed processing (Hadoop, Spark).

## Part D: Applications and Analysis (Q31-40)

### Q31. You want to study customer retention but only have transaction data. What can you do?
**Model answer:** Behavioural analysis: (1) Compute customer tenure, purchase frequency, monetary value (RFM). (2) Identify churn events (no purchase in X months). (3) Model hazard rate with survival analysis. (4) Segment customers by behaviour. (5) Correlate with internal metrics (pricing, promotions). Limitations: no attitudinal data (why they churn). Supplement with survey of churned customers.

### Q32. What is the challenge of using Twitter/social media data in research?
**Model answer:** (1) Self-selected sample — not representative of population. (2) Platform biases (age, education of users). (3) API rate limits restrict data volume. (4) Terms of Service may prohibit specific analyses. (5) Privacy concerns for individual users. (6) Text is informal, with slang/emojis. (7) Bot activity contaminates data. Report limitations prominently.

### Q33. Describe sentiment analysis.
**Model answer:** Computational extraction of subjective information from text — positive, negative, neutral. Methods: (1) Lexicon-based — match words to sentiment dictionary (LIWC, VADER). (2) Machine learning — train classifier on labelled data (SVM, neural networks). (3) Hybrid. Used for: brand perception, product reviews, political sentiment.

### Q34. What is content analysis?
**Model answer:** Systematic, replicable analysis of text/image content. Two types: (1) Quantitative — count word or code frequencies, statistically analyse. (2) Qualitative — interpret themes and meanings. Steps: define categories, code sample, ensure reliability, analyse patterns. Applied to media, speeches, interviews, documents.

### Q35. Compare qualitative and quantitative content analysis.
**Model answer:** Quantitative: counts and frequencies, statistical testing, replicability key. Example: counting negative words in headlines. Qualitative: interpretive, meaning-focused, flexible. Example: themes in how CEOs frame strategy. Both can coexist in a single study.

### Q36. Describe the use of government statistics in market research.
**Model answer:** Government data: census (demographics, housing), labour statistics (employment, wages), trade data, consumer expenditure. Free and comprehensive. Uses: (1) sizing markets, (2) understanding context, (3) weighting survey data, (4) validating findings. Limitations: infrequent collection, aggregated categories, reporting delays.

### Q37. How do you integrate secondary data with primary data?
**Model answer:** Steps: (1) Identify research gaps secondary data fills — avoid collecting redundant data. (2) Match variable definitions across sources. (3) Use secondary for context, primary for focused research questions. (4) Merge carefully via common identifiers or matching. (5) Weighted analysis if samples differ in representation. (6) Report both sources transparently.

### Q38. Evaluate credibility of a commercial secondary data source.
**Model answer:** (1) Reputation — peer-reviewed, longstanding provider. (2) Methodology transparency — sampling, collection, adjustments documented. (3) Response rates and quality controls. (4) Cross-validation — does it match other sources? (5) Use in academic/industry publications. (6) Client references. Red flags: opaque methods, no response rates, "proprietary" used to hide methodology.

### Q39. What is scanner data?
**Model answer:** Point-of-sale data from retailers: product, price, volume, time, store. Rich for marketing research — price elasticity, promotion effectiveness, market share. Providers: Nielsen, IRI. Advantages: large, real, automatic. Limitations: only tracked retailers, no customer information (unless linked to loyalty cards).

### Q40. What is panel data (consumer panels)?
**Model answer:** Data where same individuals or households are observed over time. Consumer panels track purchases, media consumption, attitudes. Nielsen, Kantar provide. Uses: market share over time, cross-sectional × longitudinal analysis, tracking response to marketing. Limitations: panel fatigue, attrition, representation.

## Part E: Ethics and Quality (Q41-45)

### Q41. What are the ethical issues in qualitative research?
**Model answer:** (1) Informed consent — participants understand purpose, risks. (2) Confidentiality — mask identities, especially sensitive settings. (3) Power dynamics — researcher/participant imbalance. (4) Transparency about research purpose. (5) Right to withdraw. (6) Beneficence — benefits for participants. (7) Respect for culture. IRB approval essential.

### Q42. Describe GDPR considerations for secondary data.
**Model answer:** GDPR governs processing of personal data in EU. Requirements: (1) Lawful basis (consent, legitimate interest). (2) Purpose limitation. (3) Data minimisation. (4) Right to erasure. (5) Data protection impact assessment for high-risk processing. Research exemptions exist but require safeguards. Secondary data must comply with original consent terms.

### Q43. What is anonymisation vs pseudonymisation?
**Model answer:** Anonymisation: removing all identifiers, impossible to re-identify. Not covered by GDPR. Pseudonymisation: replacing identifiers with codes, with separate linkage. Still personal data under GDPR. True anonymisation difficult with rich data — re-identification risk from combining variables.

### Q44. How do you balance depth of qualitative research with generalisability?
**Model answer:** Qualitative research sacrifices generalisability for depth. To partially bridge: (1) Diverse purposive sampling — capture range of perspectives. (2) Analytical generalisation — apply theory to other contexts. (3) Mixed methods — test qualitative findings quantitatively. (4) Replication across contexts. (5) Rich description enables reader to judge transferability.

### Q45. Describe emergent quantitative methods in qualitative research.
**Model answer:** (1) Text analytics — frequencies, word clouds, topic modelling on large text corpora. (2) Network analysis — map relationships in interview networks. (3) Sentiment analysis — systematic sentiment scoring. (4) Qualitative comparative analysis (QCA) — formalise configurational causality. Useful for large qualitative datasets or mixed methods.

## Part F: Application (Q46-50)

### Q46. Design a qualitative study to understand why customers churn from a streaming service.
**Model answer:** (1) Purposive sample of 25-30 churned customers with diverse demographics and tenure. (2) Semi-structured depth interviews (45 min): reasons for signup, experience, triggers for cancellation. (3) Follow-up focus groups with 8 participants each to explore themes. (4) Thematic analysis: open coding, theme generation. (5) Triangulate with usage data from the company. (6) Deliverable: priority-ranked list of churn drivers with illustrative quotes. Recommendations for retention strategy.

### Q47. You have 2 million customer reviews. How do you extract insights?
**Model answer:** (1) Preprocess text: tokenisation, lemmatisation, stopword removal. (2) Topic modelling (LDA) — identify main themes automatically. (3) Sentiment analysis per topic — positive/negative per theme. (4) Frequency and co-occurrence analysis. (5) Drill-down: sample and read reviews in each topic for context. (6) Time series: how do themes/sentiment evolve? (7) Competitor comparison if available. (8) Actionable segmentation (high-volume complaints vs niche issues).

### Q48. A client wants to use government census data for segmentation. Walk through the process.
**Model answer:** (1) Identify relevant census variables: age, income, household type, ethnicity, region. (2) Download at appropriate geographic unit (postcode sector, LSOA). (3) Cluster analysis to identify neighbourhood segments. (4) Validate with known types (urban/rural, affluent/deprived). (5) Overlay client's customer database by geography. (6) Compare client customer profile to area profiles — identify opportunities/gaps. (7) Recommendations: where to target, which segments to grow. (8) Acknowledge: area-level (ecological fallacy if applied to individuals).

### Q49. Design a study combining qualitative and quantitative methods (sequential explanatory design).
**Model answer:** Phase 1 (quant): Online survey of 1,000 customers on satisfaction. Statistical analysis identifies low-satisfaction segments. Phase 2 (qual): Depth interviews (n = 20) with low-satisfaction customers from Phase 1. Explore drivers of dissatisfaction qualitatively. Thematic analysis identifies root causes. Integration: quant identifies "what" and "who", qual explains "why". Final report: triangulated insights for management action. Strength: combines breadth and depth.

### Q50. Critique the statement: "We did 10 interviews so our sample is too small."
**Model answer:** In qualitative research, sample size is judged by saturation and richness, not statistical power. 10 in-depth interviews may be sufficient if: (1) Participants are diverse on key dimensions. (2) Saturation reached — no new themes emerge. (3) Research question is focused. For exploratory research, 8-12 interviews often sufficient. If critique stems from quantitative thinking, reframe: qualitative seeks depth and theoretical generalisation, not statistical representation. Address with rich description and connection to theory.

---

**Exam tip:** For qualitative questions, always: (1) justify method choice against the research question, (2) describe sampling strategy (purposive, theoretical) and justify n, (3) explain coding/analysis approach with worked example, (4) discuss rigour criteria (credibility, transferability, dependability), (5) address reflexivity and ethical considerations. For secondary data: assess fit, currency, accuracy, and source credibility explicitly.
