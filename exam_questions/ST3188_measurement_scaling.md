# ST3188 — Measurement & Scaling: 50 Exam Questions with Model Answers

---

## Part A: Measurement Fundamentals (Q1-10)

### Q1. Define measurement.
**Model answer:** Assigning numbers to objects or events according to rules, to represent attributes of interest. Moves from abstract constructs (satisfaction, intelligence) to observable scores. Quality depends on validity (measures what it claims) and reliability (consistent results).

### Q2. State the four levels of measurement (Stevens' scales).
**Model answer:** (1) Nominal — categories without order (gender, brand). (2) Ordinal — ordered categories without equal intervals (preference rank, agreement scale). (3) Interval — equal intervals, arbitrary zero (temperature °C, IQ). (4) Ratio — equal intervals, true zero (weight, income). Determines permissible statistics.

### Q3. What statistics are permissible for each measurement level?
**Model answer:** Nominal: mode, frequencies, chi-squared. Ordinal: median, percentiles, rank-based tests (Mann-Whitney). Interval: mean, SD, Pearson correlation, t-tests, ANOVA. Ratio: all above plus ratio calculations (twice as much). Higher levels permit all lower-level operations plus more.

### Q4. Distinguish categorical, ordinal, interval, ratio with examples.
**Model answer:** Categorical (nominal): blood type, religion. Ordinal: education level (high school, bachelor, masters), rank in class. Interval: Celsius, calendar years, IQ. Ratio: age in years, weight, number of children, income. Ratio differs from interval by having a meaningful zero (no income = truly no income).

### Q5. Define construct.
**Model answer:** Abstract theoretical concept not directly observable but inferred from behaviour or responses. Examples: intelligence, satisfaction, personality. Constructs are operationalised through scales that measure observable indicators.

### Q6. What is operationalisation?
**Model answer:** Translating a construct into specific measurable items. Example: "job satisfaction" → 5-item scale with questions about pay, environment, colleagues, growth, management. Operationalisation choices affect validity — poor operationalisation yields results of uncertain meaning.

### Q7. State Crocker and Algina's dimensions of measurement quality.
**Model answer:** Validity (accuracy, what is measured) and reliability (consistency, how consistently measured). Both necessary: reliable-but-invalid means consistently wrong; valid-but-unreliable means occasionally right with lots of noise. Goal: high on both.

### Q8. Distinguish reliability types.
**Model answer:** (1) Test-retest — same measurement at two time points. (2) Inter-rater — two observers agree. (3) Parallel forms — two equivalent versions give similar scores. (4) Internal consistency — items within a scale correlate (Cronbach's α). Each type addresses a different kind of consistency.

### Q9. State validity types.
**Model answer:** (1) Face validity — appears to measure what it should. (2) Content validity — covers all aspects of construct. (3) Criterion validity — correlates with external criterion (concurrent or predictive). (4) Construct validity — relates to theoretical network (convergent, discriminant). (5) Ecological validity — generalises to real-world contexts.

### Q10. What is measurement error?
**Model answer:** Difference between observed score (X) and true score (T). X = T + e. Random error (e) averages to zero, reduces reliability. Systematic error introduces bias, reduces validity. Total variance = true score variance + error variance. Reliability = true score variance / total variance.

## Part B: Scaling Techniques (Q11-20)

### Q11. What is a scale?
**Model answer:** A set of items (statements, questions) designed to measure a construct, yielding a composite score. Scales can be single-item (simpler but noisier) or multi-item (more reliable, covers construct better). Standard scales are validated instruments with established psychometric properties.

### Q12. Describe the Likert scale.
**Model answer:** Summated rating scale where respondents indicate level of agreement (typically 5 or 7 points). Items should measure single construct; half positive, half reversed. Scale score = sum or mean of item scores. Treat as ordinal strictly; often treated as interval for analysis.

### Q13. Describe the semantic differential scale.
**Model answer:** Bipolar adjective pairs (e.g., "good __ __ __ __ __ bad") with respondent marking position between. Multiple item pairs per concept. Advantages: quick, measures connotative meaning. Used in brand imagery, attitude research. Osgood's three underlying dimensions: evaluation, potency, activity.

### Q14. Compare Likert vs semantic differential.
**Model answer:** Likert: agreement with statements, agreement-focused. Semantic differential: bipolar adjectives, connotative focus. Both are summated scales; both produce composite scores. Likert more common for attitudes/behaviours; semantic differential more common for image and feeling.

### Q15. Describe a Guttman scale.
**Model answer:** Hierarchical scale where items are ordered by difficulty. If respondent agrees with difficult item, should agree with all easier items. Coefficient of reproducibility > 0.90 = good scale. Rare in practice because few constructs are truly unidimensional and hierarchical.

### Q16. Describe Thurstone scaling.
**Model answer:** Experts rate each potential item's position on a favourability scale. Items with high agreement selected. Respondent endorses subset; score = mean scale value of endorsed items. Elaborate development; rarely used today but historically foundational.

### Q17. What is a bipolar vs unipolar scale?
**Model answer:** Bipolar: spans from negative to positive with neutral midpoint (e.g., "strongly disagree to strongly agree"). Unipolar: spans from none to maximum (e.g., "not at all to extremely"). Choose bipolar when respondents may differ in direction; unipolar when only magnitude matters.

### Q18. How do you determine the optimal number of scale points?
**Model answer:** Common: 5 or 7 points. Trade-off: more points = finer discrimination but more cognitive effort and response styles. Research suggests 7 points optimal for most adults. Odd (with neutral) vs even (forced choice) depends on whether neutrality is meaningful. Fewer points for telephone administration.

### Q19. Should you include a "don't know" option?
**Model answer:** Advantages: reduces random responses when genuinely unsure. Disadvantages: many use as satisficing option (lowest effort). Recommendation: offer if topic may be unknown; omit if expected knowledge is baseline. Track its frequency to assess question quality.

### Q20. What is a forced-choice format?
**Model answer:** Respondent must choose between options (e.g., "Which do you prefer, A or B?" — no "either" option). Used for preferences, trade-offs, ipsative measurement. Prevents central tendency bias. Limits: cannot capture indifference; may force artificial distinctions.

## Part C: Reliability Measurement (Q21-30)

### Q21. State Cronbach's alpha formula.
**Model answer:** α = (k/(k−1)) · [1 − Σσᵢ² / σ_T²] where k = number of items, σᵢ² = variance of item i, σ_T² = variance of total scale score. Measures internal consistency. Range [0, 1]. Sensitive to item count — more items → higher α (if correlated).

### Q22. Interpret Cronbach's alpha values.
**Model answer:** α ≥ 0.9: excellent (but may suggest redundancy). 0.8 ≤ α < 0.9: good. 0.7 ≤ α < 0.8: acceptable. 0.6 ≤ α < 0.7: questionable. α < 0.6: poor. Note: high α does not imply unidimensionality — items may measure multiple correlated factors.

### Q23. What is test-retest reliability and how to assess?
**Model answer:** Correlation between scale scores at two time points from same respondents. r > 0.70 acceptable. Requires stable construct and appropriate time gap (short enough to preserve state, long enough to avoid memory). Sensitive to practice effects and true change over time.

### Q24. Describe inter-rater reliability.
**Model answer:** Consistency of ratings between multiple raters. Cohen's κ for categorical: κ = (p_o − p_e)/(1 − p_e), chance-corrected agreement. Intraclass correlation (ICC) for continuous. Benchmarks: κ > 0.61 substantial, > 0.81 almost perfect. Critical for subjective ratings (coding, diagnosis).

### Q25. What is parallel forms reliability?
**Model answer:** Correlation between scores on two equivalent versions of a test. Requires developing two versions with similar content and difficulty. Strengths: separates true score stability from measurement error. Weaknesses: resource-intensive to create genuinely equivalent forms.

### Q26. Describe split-half reliability.
**Model answer:** Divide items into two halves (e.g., odd-even), compute correlation between halves, apply Spearman-Brown correction to estimate full-scale reliability: r_SB = 2r/(1+r). Estimates internal consistency without requiring repeat administration. Historical; Cronbach's α generally preferred.

### Q27. What is the Spearman-Brown prophecy formula?
**Model answer:** Estimates reliability change with scale length: r_new = (n·r_old)/(1 + (n−1)·r_old), where n is the ratio of new length to old. Adding items increases reliability but with diminishing returns. Useful for planning scale length given acceptable reliability.

### Q28. Why does scale length affect reliability?
**Model answer:** More items = more sampling of the construct = less influence of any single item's measurement error. Averaging more items reduces random error. Mathematically: reliability increases monotonically with number of equivalent items. Trade-off: longer scales increase participant burden.

### Q29. What is item-total correlation?
**Model answer:** Correlation between an item's score and the total scale score (excluding that item, called "corrected"). Indicates item's contribution to scale. r < 0.30 suggests weak item — consider revision or removal. Key diagnostic during scale development.

### Q30. Explain the relationship between validity and reliability.
**Model answer:** Reliability is necessary but not sufficient for validity. Unreliable measure cannot be valid (consistently inconsistent). Reliable measure may or may not be valid — could consistently measure wrong thing. Maximum possible validity is bounded by √reliability. Aim for high on both.

## Part D: Validity Testing (Q31-40)

### Q31. Describe convergent validity.
**Model answer:** Scale correlates with other measures of the same construct. Example: new depression scale correlates strongly with Beck Depression Inventory. High convergent validity supports that the scale measures the intended construct. Expected correlations: > 0.50 between same-construct measures.

### Q32. Describe discriminant validity.
**Model answer:** Scale does NOT correlate strongly with measures of different constructs. Example: depression scale should correlate less with anxiety scale than with another depression scale. Expected: correlations with unrelated constructs should be modest (< 0.30). Together with convergent, establishes construct validity.

### Q33. What is the Multitrait-Multimethod Matrix (MTMM)?
**Model answer:** Campbell-Fiske method for construct validity: measure multiple traits using multiple methods. Matrix shows correlations. Convergent: same trait-different method (should be high). Discriminant: different trait-same method (should be lower than convergent, showing method doesn't drive correlation).

### Q34. Describe criterion validity.
**Model answer:** Scale scores relate to an external criterion. Types: (1) Concurrent — measured at same time (depression scale vs clinical interview today). (2) Predictive — scale predicts future (intelligence test predicts school performance). High correlation with criterion = good criterion validity.

### Q35. What is predictive validity?
**Model answer:** Scale scores predict future outcomes. Example: personality measure predicts job performance; GRE predicts grad school success. Tested by collecting scale now and outcome later. Critical for selection and forecasting instruments.

### Q36. What is content validity?
**Model answer:** Scale items adequately cover all aspects of the construct. Established by expert judgement: subject matter experts rate items for relevance and comprehensiveness. Content Validity Index (CVI): proportion of experts rating item as relevant. Key for constructs with defined domains.

### Q37. Describe convergent and discriminant validity tests.
**Model answer:** (1) Compute correlations between scale and related constructs (should be high). (2) Correlations with unrelated constructs (should be low). (3) For factor analysis, items should load highly on intended factor, low on others. (4) Structural equation modelling formalises these tests. Document during scale development.

### Q38. What is confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) and its role in scale validation?
**Model answer:** Tests whether observed items conform to hypothesised factor structure. Fit indices: CFI, TLI (≥ 0.95), RMSEA (≤ 0.06), SRMR (≤ 0.08). Used to validate existing scales or confirm structure after EFA. Key for publishing scale development research.

### Q39. Describe exploratory factor analysis (EFA) and when to use.
**Model answer:** Identifies underlying factor structure without pre-specifying. Extract factors (PCA or common factor analysis), rotate for interpretability (varimax, promax). Used in scale development to group items into subscales. Followed by CFA on new sample for confirmation.

### Q40. Explain the role of pilot testing in scale development.
**Model answer:** Pre-test with small sample (~50-100) to: (1) check item comprehension, (2) estimate reliability, (3) identify problematic items, (4) refine wording, (5) verify factor structure. Iterative process — may require several rounds of revision. Skipping pilot is a common cause of failed full-scale studies.

## Part E: Reliability and Validity Calculations (Q41-45)

### Q41. A 10-item scale has α = 0.75. Estimate α for a 15-item version with similar items.
**Model answer:** Use Spearman-Brown: r_new = (1.5 × 0.75)/(1 + 0.5 × 0.75) = 1.125/1.375 = 0.82. Adding 5 items raises reliability from 0.75 to approximately 0.82.

### Q42. Cohen's kappa: two raters classify 100 items, agreement = 85%, expected agreement = 60%. Compute κ.
**Model answer:** κ = (0.85 − 0.60)/(1 − 0.60) = 0.25/0.40 = 0.625. Substantial agreement (Landis-Koch scale: 0.61-0.80 substantial). Better than chance but room for improvement — investigate disagreements.

### Q43. Test-retest correlation = 0.80 at 2-week intervals. Interpret.
**Model answer:** Strong stability — the construct is measured consistently over time. Scores are reliable. Note: 20% of variance is due to time-varying factors (true change or measurement error). For a stable construct (e.g., personality), 0.80 is acceptable; for a state (mood), lower is expected.

### Q44. Scale validity: convergent r = 0.70, discriminant r = 0.35. Evaluate.
**Model answer:** Convergent > discriminant as required. However, discriminant r = 0.35 is moderate — perhaps higher than desired. Investigate: do the two constructs genuinely overlap, or is the scale not sufficiently specific? May require item refinement to improve discriminant validity. Overall, acceptable evidence with room for improvement.

### Q45. In an MTMM matrix: same trait/different method = 0.60; different trait/same method = 0.50; different trait/different method = 0.20. Interpret.
**Model answer:** Convergent validity acceptable (0.60). Concerning: different trait/same method (0.50) is close to convergent (0.60), suggesting method effect may inflate same-method correlations. Discriminant validity (0.20 different everything) is strong, confirming constructs are distinct. The method effect should be addressed in scale design.

## Part F: Application (Q46-50)

### Q46. Develop a 7-item scale to measure "customer loyalty".
**Model answer:** (1) Define construct: emotional attachment + behavioural commitment. (2) Item pool from literature + focus groups (20-30 candidate items). (3) Items: "I would recommend X to others" (advocacy), "I would choose X over competitors" (preference), "I identify with the brand" (identification), "I am committed to X for the long term" (commitment), "X makes me happy" (emotion), plus reverse-scored items. (4) Likert 7-point scale. (5) Pilot test, compute α, item-total correlations. (6) CFA to confirm unidimensionality. (7) Test convergent validity with repeat purchase intention; discriminant with satisfaction (related but distinct). Iteratively refine.

### Q47. Your depression scale has α = 0.92 with 20 items. Should you use it?
**Model answer:** α = 0.92 excellent internal consistency. However: (1) Check item redundancy — very high α may mean items are nearly identical. (2) Shorter version (5-10 items) may maintain acceptable α with less burden. (3) Assess validity (convergent/discriminant) separately. (4) Confirm unidimensionality (not guaranteed by high α). (5) Review participant feedback for burden. If only modest improvement over 10-item version, use shorter.

### Q48. A 4-item scale measures customer satisfaction but shows α = 0.55. What do you do?
**Model answer:** α = 0.55 below acceptable threshold (0.70). Diagnostic steps: (1) Compute item-total correlations — identify weak items. (2) Check alpha-if-deleted — would removal of an item improve α? (3) Check for reverse-coded items with errors in coding. (4) Factor analysis — might items measure multiple factors? (5) Consider adding items (Spearman-Brown: adding 4 more items ~doubles effective length, could raise α to ~0.70). Do not use current scale for conclusions; revise and revalidate.

### Q49. Compare a 5-point and 7-point Likert scale for measuring job satisfaction.
**Model answer:** 5-point: easier for respondents, acceptable for quick surveys, less fine-grained. 7-point: better discrimination, more variance captured, statistical power marginally higher. Research: little difference in psychometrics beyond 7 points. Mode matters: phone = fewer points (5); web/paper = more (7). Cultural: some cultures favour or avoid extremes differently. Consistent within study is more important than choice between 5 and 7.

### Q50. Describe a validation study for a new workplace engagement scale.
**Model answer:** (1) Define engagement theoretically; generate 30 items. (2) Expert review for content validity; refine to 20 items. (3) Pilot on 100 employees — compute α, item-total correlations, EFA. Retain 12 items with best psychometrics. (4) Main study (n = 500): compute α, test-retest reliability (n = 100 subsample at 2 weeks). (5) Validate: (a) convergent (correlate with established measures), (b) discriminant (against distinct constructs), (c) criterion (predicts turnover, performance). (6) CFA to confirm factor structure. (7) Cross-validation on independent sample. (8) Publish scale with full psychometric report. Typical duration: 12-18 months.

---

**Exam tip:** For measurement/scaling questions, always: (1) identify measurement level (nominal, ordinal, interval, ratio), (2) justify scale type (Likert, semantic differential) against the construct, (3) evaluate reliability (α, test-retest, inter-rater) and validity (convergent, discriminant, criterion), (4) provide specific diagnostic tests and acceptable thresholds, (5) describe pilot testing and refinement process.
